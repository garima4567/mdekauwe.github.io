Research

My research focuses on improving our understanding of land-atmosphere interactions, with an emphasis on forest ecosystems and their responses to environmental change. I am particularly interested in ways to utilise experimental and satellite data to develop more evidence based models.

In 2008, I completed my PhD at UCL, under the supervision of Philip Lewis, Mat Disney and Mathew Williams. My PhD involved implementing a data assimilation scheme to combine satellite derived observations of the land surface with a simple carbon balance ecosystem model (DALEC).

After completing my PhD I moved to the Centre for Ecology and Hydrology (CEH) to take up a position with the grand job title "Earth Observation and Land Surface Modeller". Here, I worked with Chris Taylor, applying a spectral analysis approach to satellite data, to characterise land surface responses to rainfall events in West Africa.

In 2010, I moved to the bottom of the globe (from my perspective!), where I have been employed as a postdoc in Belinda Medlyn's Climate and Forest Ecosystem Modelling (CAFE M) lab. At Macquarie University, I have been testing a suite of ecosystem models with data from a series of Free-Air CO2 Enrichment (FACE) experiments, both from the US and here in Australia. The ultimate goal is to improve terrestrial biosphere models (e.g. the Generic Decomposition and Yield, GDAY model), such that they more accurately represent elevated CO2 effects on vegetation. Recently, I have been collaborating with Andy Pitman's group at the University of New South Wales. Here the focus has been on improvements to the Community Atmosphere Biosphere Land Exchange (CABLE) land surface model.

Curently, I am working on identifying regions of New South Wales (Australia) where tree species may be susceptible to drought mortality. The aim of this project is to critical examine drought vunerability for key tree species in light of projected changes in precipitation and temperature.
